How to Practice Running


For intermediate-advanced runners.
As runners ease into a regular running schedule, how can they take steps to improve their speed or endurance?

TOP TIP

If you have a race coming up, it is better to start ramping up the intensity and training as slowly and early as possible, such as at least many weeks or months before. As you ramp up your speed and/or distance, be sure to pay attention to how comfortable or uncomfortable your body is after running more and more. Another note: always, always warm up AND cool down after a run.

EXAMPLE SCHEDULE

Mon: long run 2-6 mi (w/ hills)
Tues: easy run 2-4 mi
Wed: run-jog alternate (1000R/1000J/800R/1000J/600R)
Thurs: 20-30min run + 4x200m w/ 2 min rest
Fri: run/walk 20-30 min
Sat: cross-train (swim, bike, etc) + core/arm workout
Sun: rest

GRADUAL INTENSITY

Ramp up intensity in 1-2 of the following ways per 2-3 weeks or a month:
- speed (by going 90% instead of 80% effort)
- # miles (by +2mi/wk)
- # days running in a week (by 1 day)

BE CAREFUL

If at any time you feel persistent pain in your shins, knees, feet, etc., you may have a running injury. Depending on the severity, we recommend you to:
- decrease mileage, speed, frequency of runs
- stop running & only cross-train
- stop running & only walk
- rest your leg with the RICE method (rest, ice, compression, elevation
- see a sports doctor/therapist


*A note about hill running. Because of its high intensity, it's best to introduce elevation running when you are already comfortable at your current weekly mileage. When running up hills, be sure to bring your knees higher and don't run hills for too long at first.

Remember to Warm Up + Cool Down

Example Warm Up/Cool Down Schedule

Warm Up Cool Down
0.5-1 mi slow jog 200m Strides (kick legs up high)
10 min leg swings, leg lifts, jumping jacks, stand up leg stretches, lunges, jumping jacks Slow jog 0.5-1 mi
Start running workout slowly then increase intensity Stretching on the ground - toe touches, etc
